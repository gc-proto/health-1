Symptoms of flu

Some people get mildly ill, while others get very sick.

Flu symptoms may include:

  • sudden and high fever (39°C and above)
  • headache
  • general aches and pain
  • fatigue (tiredness)
  • chills
  • loss of appetite
  • runny or stuffy nose
  • sore throat
  • cough
  • stomach ache
  • diarrhea
  • nausea and vomiting

Symptoms appear 1 to 4 days after exposure to the virus.

Flu vs a cold

These symptoms usually tell you have a flu, and not the common cold:

  • high fever
  • headache
  • muscle aches and pains
Symptom Flu (influenza) Cold
Fever Usual, sudden high fever, lasts 3-4 days Rare
Headache Usual, can be severe Rare
General aches and pains Usual, often severe Sometimes, mild
Tired and weak Usual, may last 2-3 weeks or more Sometimes, mild
Extreme fatigue Usual, early onset Unusual
Runny, stuffy nose Common Common
Sneezing Sometimes Common
Sore throat Common Common
Chest discomfort, coughing Usual, can be severe Sometimes, mild to moderate

If you become ill

Most people recover from the flu in 7 to 10 days. Others may develop serious complications, such as pneumonia (a lung infection), and may need hospital care.

If you are mildly ill, stay home and avoid contact with other people until your symptoms are gone. This will help prevent the spread of the virus.

If you are a person at high risk of flu-related complications, contact your health care provider. Tell them about your symptoms.

Contagious period

People infected with the flu virus can spread it to others:

  • starting 1 day before the first symptoms
  • until approximately 5 days after the first symptoms

When to see a health care provider

If you develop any of the following symptoms:

  • Shortness of breath, rapid breathing or difficulty breathing
  • Chest pain
  • Bluish or grey skin colour
  • Bloody or coloured mucus/spit
  • Sudden dizziness or confusion
  • Severe or persistent vomiting
  • High fever (39°C and above) lasting more than three days
  • Low blood pressure

Tell your health care provider about your flu symptoms over the phone before your appointment. That way, they can arrange to see you without exposing other people to the virus.

Additional symptoms to watch for in children

  • Not drinking or eating as usual
  • Not waking up or interacting with others
  • Irritable (not wanting to play or be held)

Possible complications

  • Can lead to pneumonia and respiratory failure
  • Can worsen a current chronic respiratory condition
  • Can be life-threatening

How the flu is diagnosed

The flu is usually diagnosed by a health care provider based on:

  • symptoms
  • laboratory tests

Treating the flu

Mild flu symptoms can be treated with:

  • rest
  • fluids
  • medicine to reduce any fever or aches

Over-the-counter cough and flu medicine should not be given to children younger than 6 years old. It is only safe to do so if advised by your health care provider.

Your health care provider may prescribe an antiviral drug if you are:

  • at high risk for flu-related complications
  • sick with more severe symptoms

It is important to start antiviral medication as soon as possible.
